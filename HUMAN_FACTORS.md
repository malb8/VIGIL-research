# Human Factors and Workflow Psychology

## The core problem

Traditional phishing awareness programs often assume that successful phishing is primarily caused by a lack of user knowledge. Users are expected to inspect URLs, recognize suspicious emails and identify obvious warning signs before interacting with a link.

Modern phishing campaigns increasingly challenge that assumption.

Many successful attacks today target employees who understand phishing, regularly complete awareness training and are fully capable of recognizing classic indicators of fraud. Yet they still make decisions that lead to credential theft, OAuth abuse or unintended access delegation.

The issue is often not a failure of knowledge.

It is a consequence of how people interact with familiar workflows.

## Trust inside familiar workflows

Most employees spend their day moving between a relatively small set of applications and processes. They triage email, review tickets, approve requests, collaborate in Teams or Slack, access cloud services through single sign-on and respond to notifications generated by business systems.

These activities are repeated hundreds or even thousands of times over months and years.

As familiarity increases, users naturally become more efficient. They stop consciously evaluating every step and instead rely on established expectations about how a workflow normally behaves. This is not negligence; it is a normal and necessary adaptation that allows people to work productively in complex digital environments.

The result is that trust gradually becomes attached to the workflow itself.

When a navigation appears to originate from a familiar process, users often inherit that trust without consciously reassessing the destination. The workflow feels legitimate, so the next action feels legitimate as well.

This transfer of trust can occur even when the final destination is controlled by an attacker.

## Why traditional phishing training has limits

Conventional awareness programs remain valuable, but they are largely built around indicators that were common in earlier phishing campaigns: suspicious domains, spelling mistakes, unexpected attachments or obviously fraudulent messages.

Those indicators still exist, but modern attacks increasingly operate inside legitimate platforms, trusted SaaS environments and authentic business processes.

As a result, users are often confronted with situations where the workflow itself appears normal. The attacker is no longer asking the victim to ignore obvious warning signs. Instead, the attacker attempts to blend into existing operational patterns and leverage trust that already exists.

In these scenarios, the challenge is less about detecting malicious content and more about recognizing when trust is being transferred from one context to another without deliberate verification.

## Human factors and security

This area overlaps with several well-established concepts from psychology and human factors research, including habituation, automatic behavior, cognitive load, attentional limitations and decision fatigue.

A useful way to think about the problem is through the distinction between fast and deliberate decision-making. Most routine workflow interactions are handled quickly because they have been performed many times before. Under normal circumstances this is highly efficient.

The downside is that moments requiring careful evaluation can become difficult to distinguish from routine operational activity.

An attacker only needs a small number of these moments to succeed.

## The design philosophy behind VIGIL Gate

VIGIL Gate was created around a simple observation: trust transitions are often invisible.

When trust moves from a familiar workflow to a browser navigation, login flow or external destination, users rarely receive an explicit signal that a reassessment may be appropriate.

VIGIL attempts to make those moments visible.

Rather than classifying websites as safe or malicious, the extension focuses on introducing a deliberate pause when a navigation appears to originate from a workflow that could influence user trust. The objective is not to replace user judgment, but to create an opportunity for that judgment to be exercised consciously.

In that sense, VIGIL is not a trust engine.

It is a workflow-aware verification checkpoint intended to make trust transitions more visible before navigation continues.
